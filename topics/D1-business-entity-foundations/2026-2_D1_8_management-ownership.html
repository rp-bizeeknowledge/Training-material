Business Management & Ownership

LLC Styles · Corporation Hierarchy · Nonprofit Governance · Special Structures
Overview

LLCs, corporations, and nonprofits each have different management structures, terminology, and state-specific rules. This overview will help you clearly understand the differences and similarities to be able to support clients through our platform.

Some states don't keep a record of management or ownership information (meaning they don't list it in the Articles of Formation), and certain management structures are not permitted in specific jurisdictions. Our platform is not designed to accommodate special configurations such as nominee managers or directors, nor does it support assigning foreign addresses to any owner, manager, or officer.

Ownership of business entities can be held by:

👤 Individuals

Natural persons acting as owners, members, or shareholders.

🏢 Companies

Legal entities such as corporations, LLCs, or partnerships that can own other entities.

Both individuals and companies may serve as owners, members, or shareholders, depending on the entity type and state law.

5.1 — Limited Liability Company (LLC)

LLCs offer flexible management options tailored to the owners' preferences. Ownership is held by either members or managers, who can be individuals or other companies, depending on state law.

Management Styles

👥 Member-Managed LLC

Definition

All owners (called members) directly handle daily business operations and decisions.

Common Use

Ideal for small LLCs where all members want to actively participate.

Decision Making

Decisions are collectively made by all members, reflecting a partnership-like structure where each member typically has equal say unless otherwise agreed.

Example Wording
"The LLC will be managed by its members."

🏢 Manager-Managed LLC

Definition

Members appoint one or more managers, who may or may not be members, to manage daily operations.

Common Use

Suited for LLCs where some members prefer a passive role or where professional management is needed.

Decision Making

Designated managers make operational decisions independently, while members retain ownership control but generally do not participate in daily management.

Example Wording
"The LLC will be managed by one manager" or "The LLC will be managed by more than one manager."
Nominee Managers
⚙️

Nominee managers are individuals appointed to act as the official managers of an LLC on paper, often to provide privacy or anonymity for the actual owners. While some LLCs may use nominee managers legally, our ordering process does not support listing managers as nominees. This means that although nominee arrangements may be allowed by law, our platform requires that the managers listed are the real, authorized representatives and does not accommodate nominee appointments.

State Regulation

Most states allow LLCs to choose the management method or designation. Some require specifying it in the formation documents. Member-managed LLCs are usually simpler and less costly, while manager-managed LLCs are preferred for larger or more complex businesses. States also vary significantly in their rules regarding membership transfers and the roles and powers of managers versus members, affecting control and ownership flexibility.

By default, all LLC filings processed through our services are set up as member-managed. However, customers might request to change this structure before the LLC is formed.

5.2 — Corporation

Corporations follow a formal, hierarchical management system established by corporate law.

Board of Directors
Role

The board of directors is elected by shareholders and serves as the corporation's governing body. Directors have fiduciary duties to act in the best interest of shareholders by setting policies, approving major business decisions, and overseeing management.

Decision Making

The board meets regularly to vote on key matters such as budgets, mergers, executive hires, and dividend declarations. Decisions require a quorum and typically pass by majority vote.

Board Decisions
Approving annual budgets and business plans
Hiring or removing the CEO and corporate officers
Approving mergers, acquisitions, or the issuance of stock
Declaring dividends to shareholders
Responsibility

Directors protect shareholder interests, ensuring the corporation complies with legal requirements and operates profitably.

Corporate Officers
📌

Role: Officers (such as President, Secretary, Treasurer) manage day-to-day business under the board's oversight.
Note: Our platform accommodates the offices of President, Secretary, and Treasurer only, although corporations may have other officer titles in practice.
Typical Flow: Shareholders → Board of Directors → Officers.

Nominee Directors
⚙️

Some corporations may have nominee directors, but we do not accommodate nominee directors in our ordering process. While nominee director arrangements exist legally, our platform does not support listing nominee directors.

State Notes

Most states require corporations to have a board of directors and officers, hold regular meetings, keep minutes, and follow corporate formalities. Requirements vary; for example, Delaware mandates at least one director, while some states allow a sole individual to serve as both director and officer.

5.3 — Nonprofit

Nonprofits have governance designed to ensure mission-driven accountability rather than profit distribution.

Board of Directors or Trustees
Role

The nonprofit board governs activities to ensure alignment with the organization's charitable mission and bylaws. Members have fiduciary duties, including governance, compliance, financial oversight, and policy-making.

Decision Making

Board decisions are made in formal meetings with votes on matters such as budgets, fundraising policies, program changes, and executive oversight. Minutes document decisions for accountability.

Board Decisions
Approving grant applications or major fundraising campaigns
Setting ethical fundraising policies and conflict of interest rules
Hiring or evaluating the Executive Director or CEO
Approving changes to programs or mission-related activities
Executive Director or CEO

Role: Appointed by the board, this individual manages day-to-day operations and staff in accordance with the nonprofit's mission.

Membership

Some nonprofits have a membership base that elects the board, while others have self-perpetuating boards.

State Regulation

Nonprofits must comply with state laws governing charitable organizations, including reporting and governance standards. Many states require boards to meet minimum size requirements (usually at least three members). Profit distribution to members is prohibited, and restrictions apply to asset use to maintain tax-exempt status.

5.4 — Special Business Structures

☂️ Umbrella Company

A business entity that provides administrative services to multiple subsidiary businesses or contractors under a single corporate structure. Often manages invoicing, payroll, and tax withholdings for contractors or multiple smaller businesses. Common in industries like staffing, freelance work, and contracting.

🏦 Holding Company

A business entity created specifically to own shares or interests in other companies rather than produce goods or services directly. Main purposes include control, risk management, and tax and financial planning. Can own one or more subsidiaries in various entity forms.

5.5 — Summary
Key Takeaway

Some states require the disclosure of members, managers, directors, or officers during formation or annual filings, while others do not. Ownership restrictions also vary: nonprofits cannot distribute profits to members, whereas LLCs and corporations have different ownership structures that must comply with state-specific transfer and ownership rules. Understanding these distinctions helps us guide customers in selecting the right entity type for their goals and ensures their formation documents meet state legal requirements. LLCs offer flexibility, while corporations and nonprofits have more formal governance and compliance frameworks, each suited to different purposes.

Important: Although nominee managers or directors may be permitted in certain states, we do not support nominee appointments in our ordering process. Additionally, not all states require or allow every management structure mentioned, and even when legally allowed, our platform may not accommodate all variations.
