Nonprofit Organization (NFP)

Mission-Driven · Board Governance · 501(c)(3) · Internal Restrictions
What It Is

A Nonprofit Corporation is a special type of business entity created for a charitable, educational, religious, scientific, or public benefit purpose, not to earn profits for owners or shareholders. Unlike regular corporations or LLCs, a nonprofit's income must be used to further its mission, not distributed to individuals.

After formation, nonprofits have the option to apply for 501(c)(3) tax-exempt status with the IRS through Form 1023, which allows them to avoid paying federal income taxes and lets donors make tax-deductible contributions. However, we do not assist with filing tax-exemption applications, such as IRS Form 1023 or 1023-EZ. These forms typically need to be completed and filed directly by the organization's interested parties or with the help of a licensed attorney or tax professional.

Like other corporations, nonprofits offer liability protection, meaning directors, officers, and members are typically not personally responsible for the organization's debts or legal issues.

Ownership and Structure

Nonprofits are not owned. Instead, they are governed by a Board of Directors responsible for ensuring the organization stays true to its mission.

The nonprofit's internal operations are outlined in its Bylaws, and they must meet specific state and IRS requirements to maintain good standing, including:

Holding regular board meetings
Keeping meeting minutes
Filing annual reports and tax returns
Internal Restrictions

While nonprofits may have a variety of stakeholders, our internal policy only supports the formation of standard nonprofit corporations with individual directors and incorporators. As with all entity types, we sometimes support trusts in these roles during the formation process. This does not mean these structures are not allowed under state law, only that they are not always supported through our services.

Example
Real-World Scenario Cookies for Classrooms — Lily's Nonprofit

Now imagine Lily takes her cookie stand in a different direction. Instead of selling cookies for personal profit, she forms a nonprofit organization called "Cookies for Classrooms." The cookie sales are used to raise money to buy school supplies and books for local children.

During one of her events, a guest eats a cookie, has an allergic reaction, and decides to sue. Because the nonprofit is a separate legal entity, the claim would be against the organization, not Lily or her family personally. This generally helps protect personal assets, such as her family's home or car.

Unlike a for-profit business, Lily cannot keep any of the money raised. All income and donations must be used to support the nonprofit's educational mission.

This example shows how nonprofits are designed to serve a public purpose while still providing legal protection to the people who operate them.

How to Explain to Customers

A nonprofit is the right structure when your goal is to serve a mission, not earn profits. It protects the people involved and allows you to apply for tax-exempt status and receive donations.
