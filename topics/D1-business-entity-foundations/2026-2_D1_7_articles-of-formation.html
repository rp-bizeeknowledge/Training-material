Articles of Formation

The official document that confirms the creation of a business entity
Filed Articles

When a customer purchases a formation package from Bizee, it creates what we call a Main Order. This Main Order triggers the filing process with the state and results in the creation of the Filed Articles, the official certificate that serves as proof of the company's formation and succesful registration with the Secretary of State. Think of it as the birth certificate of a company.

Updating information in the Filed Articles

If a customer later needs to change the information listed on the filed articles, such as the business name, company address, or management structure, these changes cannot be made by editing or reissuing the original Filed Articles. Instead, the customer must file a separate document called an Amendment.

Think of it like buying a used car:

Filed Articles

The original vehicle title — proof that the car exists and who first owned it. It never goes away; it stays on record permanently.

Amendment

A title transfer — it updates the record to reflect what changed, but doesn't erase the original. Both documents exist side by side.

An Amendment does not replace the Filed Articles. It simply becomes an additional record in the state's system, confirming that a change has occurred. The original filing always remains on record with the state for historical and legal reference.

The Role of the Organizer

The role of the Organizer/Incorporator is limited to filing the necessary forms with the state and overseeing the formation process. Organizers are not owners or ongoing decision-makers. Once the company is formed, control automatically shifts to the actual members (LLCs) or directors (Corporations).

When a company is formed through our services, we include a Statement of the Organizer (for LLCs) or Statement of the Incorporator (for Corporations and Nonprofits). These confirm who filed the paperwork and show they stepped down after formation.

If ownership needs to be proven, the Statement, along with the Operating Agreement or corporate bylaws, can be used. This is especially helpful in states that do not list members or directors in the Articles of Organization or Incorporation.
