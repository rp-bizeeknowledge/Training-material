Limited Liability Company (LLC)

Simplicity, flexibility and liability protection
What It Is

A Limited Liability Company (LLC) is a flexible and widely used business structure that combines the benefits of a corporation and a sole proprietorship. One of its most important features is personal liability protection, which means the individuals who own the business (called members) are not personally responsible for the company's debts, legal obligations, or lawsuits. In other words, their personal assets, like their home, car, or bank account, are typically protected if the business encounters financial trouble.

Ownership in an LLC is based on membership percentages rather than stock. Each member's share is usually tied to their capital contribution or defined in the Operating Agreement.

Operating Agreement

The internal rulebook for an LLC. It outlines how the business is structured, including ownership percentages, decision-making authority, and how profits and responsibilities are handled. Its purpose is to put expectations in writing so there is clarity before any issues arise.

Members

In LLCs, the individuals who own the business are legally referred to as "members," not "owners." This terminology distinguishes LLCs from corporations, which use "shareholders," and sole proprietorships, for which the term "owner" is correct.

Membership %

Ownership is based on membership percentages rather than stock. Each member's share is usually tied to their capital contribution or defined in the Operating Agreement.

Internal Restrictions

Depending on the laws of the state where the LLC is formed, members can be individuals, corporations, other LLCs, trusts, or even foreign entities. Our formation services, however, sometimes allow trusts but unfortunately not companies formed outside of the U.S. to be listed as members during the filing process, even if the state permits it. It's important to keep this restriction in mind when assisting customers.

Example
Lily's Cookie Stand — Allergic Reaction Lawsuit

Lily starts selling homemade cookies from a front lawn. One day, a customer buys a cookie, later has an allergic reaction because it contains peanuts, and decides to sue.

❌

No LLC Formed

Personal liability may apply. That could expose homeowner's insurance, personal savings, or other personal assets to claims.

✅

LLC Formed

The claim would generally be directed at the business rather than the individual. In most cases, only the money and property owned by the business would be at risk, not personal assets such as a home or savings.

This example shows why many entrepreneurs choose an LLC. An LLC creates a legal separation between the individual and the business, helping limit personal risk if something goes wrong.
