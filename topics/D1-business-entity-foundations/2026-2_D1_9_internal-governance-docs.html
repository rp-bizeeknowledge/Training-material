FOUNDATIONS · Topic 09

Internal Governance & Banking Documents

Internal documents that guide business operations and decision-making
LLC Operating Agreement

An Operating Agreement is a private contract between the members of an LLC. It defines how the business will be managed, including ownership percentages, decision-making processes, profit and loss distribution, and what happens when a member joins or leaves. It helps set clear expectations and reduces the risk of misunderstandings among members.

We provide an Operating Agreement template with standard and premium orders. However, to be valid, it must be completed, dated, and signed by the members. Some banks or agencies may also require it to be notarized.

Importance

Even if not legally required, having an Operating Agreement is strongly recommended because it:

Protects limited liability by formally separating personal and business assets
Overrides default state laws with customized business rules
Prevents internal disputes by clarifying ownership, roles, and financial terms
State Requirements

Some states (such as California and New York) require LLCs to have an Operating Agreement. Without one, default laws will apply, which may not align with your intended structure (i.e., splitting profits equally regardless of investment).

Updates

The operating agreement can be modified if the LLC's structure or internal operations need to change. If the change affects information filed with the state (i.e., changing the LLC's management structure or registered member information), an amendment filing may be required before the update. For less formal changes, such as updating membership percentages, an internal update may be sufficient.

Corporate Governance Documents

Corporate governance documents are the core internal records that define how a corporation is structured, controlled, and operated. They establish authority, decision-making rules, accountability, and they are distinct from documents filed with the state.

Corporate Bylaws

Corporate bylaws serve as a corporation's internal rulebook, defining the roles and responsibilities of shareholders, directors, and officers. They also outline how critical decisions are made. Essentially, bylaws provide the framework for running the business, ensuring that everyone within the company is aligned with its goals and operations.

Meeting Minutes

Meeting minutes are the official record of board meetings. While the bylaws set the rules, the minutes document how those rules are applied, recording who attended, what decisions were made, and what actions were taken. These minutes provide a historical account of the company's decision-making process and serve as proof of the actions taken during meetings.

Importance

Corporate bylaws and meeting minutes are important governance documents because they:

Establish internal structure by creating a clear record of how the corporation is organized and governed
Ensure compliance from the start by formalizing roles, rules, and decision-making processes
Prove governance and ownership in states where directors are not listed in the Articles of Incorporation
State Requirements

Most states require corporations to adopt bylaws, hold regular meetings, maintain meeting minutes, and follow corporate formalities. Requirements vary by state; for example, Delaware requires corporations to maintain certain corporate records and document key business decisions. Failing to follow these formalities can put the corporation's good standing and liability protections at risk.

Updates

The bylaws can be modified if the company's structure needs to change (i.e., altering management roles or updating shareholder information). If the change involves something that needs to be listed with the state (i.e., a director change), an amendment may be required before the update. For less formal changes, such as updating shareholder information, an email may be sufficient to update the record.

Banking Resolution

The Banking Resolution is a document that authorizes an individual to open and manage a bank account on behalf of a business entity. This individual is also designated as an "authorized signer", giving them the ability to handle the bank account.

For customers who file a main order with us, the Banking Resolution is included in our Standard and Premium packages. If they did not select one of these packages, the Banking Resolution can still be added through our Business Contract Templates library for a $150 fee. It is not available as a standalone service outside these options.


Any templates provided by Bizee should be reviewed, completed, and signed by the appropriate parties for the business entity (e.g., members or managers for LLCs, directors or officers for corporations). Depending on third-party requirements (such as banks or licensing authorities), notarization may also be required.
