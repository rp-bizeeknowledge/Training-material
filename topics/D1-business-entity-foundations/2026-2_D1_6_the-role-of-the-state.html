1.5 — Designators

An entity designator is the abbreviation added to a business name to indicate its legal structure. It helps the public, customers, and government agencies identify the type of business entity you're operating.

Entity designators vary by state, and each state requires certain types to follow naming rules that include the appropriate designator. Common designations include:

Entity TypeCommon Designators
LLCLLC · L.L.C. · Ltd. Liability Co.
CorporationCorp. · Inc. · Co. · Ltd.
NonprofitInc. · NFP · Nonprofit Corporation

There are additional considerations that can be reviewed in the state-specific internal Resource Guide.

2 — The Role of the State

The state where a business is formed plays a major role in the overall cost and maintenance of the entity. From initial filing fees to annual taxes and compliance requirements, each one has its own rules that affect long-term operations.

💵 2.1 — Filing Fee

The initial cost paid to the state to legally form a business. Varies per state, non-refundable, and required to officially register LLCs, Corporations, and Nonprofits.

📋 2.3 — Annual Reports

A required filing that keeps the business in good standing with the state. Usually includes business name, address, and ownership info. Missing it can result in penalties or administrative dissolution.

📊 2.2 — State Taxes

In addition to federal taxes, many states charge businesses a recurring franchise tax, minimum tax, or annual fee simply for the right to exist or operate in that state. These fees are separate from income tax and often apply even if the business earns no revenue. They are typically due every year, and missing them can result in penalties or loss of good standing.

Beyond state-level fees, additional taxes may apply at the county or city level, depending on where the business operates. These can include local business taxes, gross receipts taxes, or licensing fees.

We cannot calculate, predict, or foresee these ongoing tax obligations, as they vary by location, business activity, and revenue. Entrepreneurs should review their specific requirements with a CPA or the appropriate state, county, or city tax authority to ensure all taxes and fees are properly handled.

Not all states charge these (e.g., no franchise tax in Wyoming)
May be based on revenue, assets, or a flat fee

🏛️ 2.4 — State Business Environment

Each state's legal and tax climate can influence a business's long-term success.

Some states offer no state income tax (e.g., Florida, Texas)
Others have business-friendly laws or better asset protection (e.g., Delaware, Wyoming)
This can affect where entrepreneurs choose to form
2.5 — We Inform, Not Advise
📌 Policy

Our role is to provide clear information, not legal or financial recommendations.

How to Explain to Customers — Filing Fees

Every state charges a fee to form a business. Some are more affordable than others, depending on where you file.

How to Explain to Customers — State Taxes

Some states require businesses to pay annual franchise taxes or fees. These costs can vary based on the size or type of the business.

How to Explain to Customers — Annual Reports

Most states require businesses to file a simple annual report to stay active. It includes basic information and typically comes with a fee.

How to Explain to Customers — State Business Environment

Certain states are considered more business-friendly due to lower taxes or easier regulations, which may be appealing depending on their goals. As a general rule, form your business in the state where you live and operate, unless you plan to raise venture capital or go public, in which case Delaware is usually the better choice.

What to Say if Asked for Advice

We can definitely explain the differences between states, but for tailored advice, we recommend speaking with a legal or tax professional.
