The Role of The State

Filing Fees · State Taxes · Annual Reports · Business Environment
HOW FORMATION STATES AFFECT BUSINESSES

Filing fees, annual reports, taxes, and compliance rules vary by state. These differences can affect both the setup process and the ongoing maintenance of a business.

Filing Fee

The one-time cost paid to the state to legally form a business. Non-refundable and required for LLCs, Corporations, and Nonprofits. Amounts vary by state.

Annual Reports

A recurring state filing that keeps a business in good standing. Includes basic information like name, address, and ownership. Missing filings can result in penalties or dissolution.

State Taxes

Many states charge a recurring franchise tax, minimum tax, or annual fee regardless of revenue. County and city-level taxes may also apply.

  • Not all states charge these (e.g., no franchise tax in Wyoming)
  • May be based on revenue, assets, or a flat fee
  • Applies to LLCs, C Corps, and sometimes Nonprofits
Business Environment

Each state's legal and tax climate can influence a business's long-term success and where entrepreneurs choose to form.

  • Some states have no state income tax (e.g., Florida, Texas)
  • Others offer business-friendly laws or stronger asset protection (e.g., Delaware, Wyoming)

We cannot calculate, predict, or foresee ongoing tax obligations — they vary by location, business activity, and revenue. Customers should consult a CPA or their state, county, or city tax authority.

We Inform, Not Advise
📌 Policy

Our role is to provide clear information, not legal or financial recommendations.

How to Explain to Customers — Filing Fees

Every state charges a fee to form a business. Some are more affordable than others, depending on where you file.

How to Explain to Customers — State Taxes

Some states require businesses to pay annual franchise taxes or fees. These costs can vary based on the size or type of the business.

How to Explain to Customers — Annual Reports

Most states require businesses to file a simple annual report to stay active. It includes basic information and typically comes with a fee.

How to Explain to Customers — State Business Environment

Certain states are considered more business-friendly due to lower taxes or easier regulations, which may be appealing depending on their goals. As a general rule, form your business in the state where you live and operate, unless you plan to raise venture capital or go public, in which case Delaware is usually the better choice.

What to Say if Asked for Advice

We can definitely explain the differences between states, but for tailored advice, we recommend speaking with a legal or tax professional.
