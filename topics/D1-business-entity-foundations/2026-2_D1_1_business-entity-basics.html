Business Entity Basics

An introduction to company structures and their characteristics
Overview

At the core of what we do, our business formation services include the preparation and filing of documents required to register the following entity types: Limited Liability Companies (LLCs), C Corporations, S Corporations, and Nonprofit Organizations. Designators

An entity designator is the abbreviation added to a business name to indicate its legal structure. It helps the public, customers, and government agencies identify the type of business entity you're operating.

Entity designators vary by state, and each state requires certain types to follow naming rules that include the appropriate designator. Common designations include:

LLC LLCL.L.C.Ltd. Liability Co.
Corporation Corp.Inc.Co.Ltd.
Nonprofit Inc.NFPNonprofit Corporation

Additional designator restrictions may apply depending on the state. State-specific guidance can be reviewed in the applicable Resource Guide within DB/Bizee Tech.

Other Specialized Entity Types (Not Supported)

There are other specialized entity types allowed under state law, including:

Professional Limited Liability Companies (PLLCs)

LLCs formed specifically for licensed professionals such as doctors, lawyers, or accountants. They offer liability protection for business debts, although each professional remains responsible for their own malpractice.

General Partnerships (GPs)

Created when two or more people agree to run a business together without forming a separate legal entity. All partners share management authority and are personally liable for the partnership's debts and obligations.

Professional Corporations (PCs)

Formed by licensed professionals who want a formal business structure while meeting state licensing requirements. They provide liability protection for business obligations, although each professional remains responsible for their own malpractice.

We do not currently support the formation of these entities. This is because they often involve state-specific licensing requirements, industry regulations, or additional documentation that fall outside the scope of our standardized filing services. While we don't file these entity types, it's useful to be familiar with them because customers sometimes ask about them.

If a customer needs to form an entity other than an LLC, C Corp, S Corp, or nonprofit, they should consult a licensed attorney or a formation provider that specializes in regulated or professional business structures.
