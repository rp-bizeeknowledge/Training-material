C Corporation

Set for growth, raising capital and formal management
What It Is

A corporation is a more structured and formal business entity that exists separately from its owners. Like an LLC, it provides limited liability protection, meaning shareholders are generally not personally responsible for the corporation’s debts, lawsuits, or legal obligations. However, corporations are subject to stricter legal, operational, and recordkeeping requirements.

Ownership in a corporation is held by shareholders through shares of stock, not membership percentages. A shareholder’s ownership rights and voting power are generally based on the number and type of shares they own. Unlike LLCs, corporations can issue multiple classes of stock, which makes them well suited for attracting investors and raising capital.

Here are key terms exclusively associated with corporations:

Articles of Incorporation

The official state filing used to legally create a corporation. This document is submitted to the state and typically includes the company name, registered agent, and authorized shares.

Stock Shares

Ownership in a corporation is divided into shares of stock. The number of shares owned generally determines ownership percentage and voting power.

Shareholders

The owners of a corporation. Ownership is represented through shares of stock rather than membership percentages. Shareholders may vote on major corporate decisions.

Bylaws & Meeting Minutes

The corporation’s internal rulebook. Bylaws define how the company is governed, including voting procedures, director responsibilities, officer roles, and meeting requirements.

Shareholders are generally not listed in public state records because ownership is considered private. Instead, individuals with management authority, such as directors or officers, may appear on filings submitted to the state.

Management Structure

Corporations follow a more formal management structure than LLCs. Oversight responsibilities are typically divided between a Board of Directors and corporate Officers, each serving different roles within the company.

Officers

Manage the corporation’s day-to-day operations. Common roles include:

  • President or CEO: oversees overall business operations and executive decision-making
  • Secretary: maintains corporate records, meeting minutes, and compliance documentation
  • Treasurer: manages financial records, banking activity, and reporting
Board of Directors

Oversee the corporation’s direction, major decisions, and long-term governance.

Directors are generally elected by shareholders and are responsible for appointing corporate officers.

In most states, the same individual can serve as Director, President, Secretary, and Treasurer simultaneously. This means one person holds responsibility for corporate governance, daily operations, recordkeeping, and financial oversight

Internal Policy

While some states allow companies formed outside the U.S. to be shareholders, our internal policy does not permit foreign entities to be listed as shareholders during the incorporation process. Trusts are always allowed. When advising customers, it's important to keep this policy in mind when explaining who can be listed as an initial shareholder on filings submitted through our services.

Example
Lily's Cookie Stand

Now let's go back to Lily and her homemade cookie stand, but this time the business is formed as a corporation before opening. To raise startup money, Lily brings in a few family members as shareholders while keeping majority ownership and control.

Later, a customer with a peanut allergy becomes ill and decides to sue. Because the cookie business is incorporated, the legal claim is generally against the corporation, not against Lily personally. In most cases, only the corporation's assets, such as its cash or baking equipment, would be at risk, not Lily's personal savings or home.

As the majority shareholder, Lily continues to run the business and can distribute profits to the other shareholders based on their stock percentages. The difference lies in how the business is structured. Unlike an LLC, a corporation has more formal requirements. Corporate records must be maintained, a defined management structure must be followed, and major decisions must be documented, even when ownership remains concentrated.

This example shows that corporations provide strong liability protection and make it easier to raise money from shareholders, but they also come with more structure and ongoing responsibilities compared to an LLC.

How to Explain to Customers

A corporation gives your business strong legal protection and is ideal if you plan to grow, raise money through investors, or operate in a more formal way. It requires more paperwork and structure than an LLC.
