C Corporation (C Corp or General Corporation)

Set for growth, raising capital and a formal management
What It Is

In contrast to an LLC, a corporation is a more formal business structure that exists as a separate legal entity from its owners. While both offer limited liability protection, corporations are governed by stricter legal, reporting, and operational requirements.

Ownership in a corporation is held by shareholders through shares of stock, not membership percentages. A shareholder's ownership interest, economic rights, and voting power are based on the number and type of shares they own. Unlike LLCs, corporations can issue multiple classes of stock, which makes them well suited for raising outside investment and scaling the business. By default, our system will assign 1,500 shares at 0.1 cents par value through the order process for a corporation.

📌

Shareholders are not listed in state records because they are considered private owners. Instead, individuals with management authority, such as directors or officers, may appear on public filings since they are responsible for running the business on record.

Management Structure

The management structure of a corporation is more formal. It typically includes:

1

Shareholders — owners of the corporation

2

Directors — elected by shareholders to oversee the company

3

Officers — such as a President or CEO, appointed to run day-to-day operations

The corporation's internal rules are set out in its Corporate Bylaws, which describe how the company is managed and how decisions are made. Meeting Minutes are used to record significant actions and decisions by shareholders and directors. These documents will be discussed in more detail later.

Internal Restrictions

While some states allow companies formed outside the U.S. to be shareholders, our internal policy does not permit foreign entities to be listed as shareholders during the incorporation process. Trusts are always allowed. When advising customers, it's important to keep this policy in mind when explaining who can be listed as an initial shareholder on filings submitted through our services.

Example
Lily's Cookie Stand

Now let's go back to Lily and her homemade cookie stand, but this time the business is formed as a corporation before opening. To raise startup money, Lily brings in a few family members as shareholders while keeping majority ownership and control.

Later, a customer with a peanut allergy becomes ill and decides to sue. Because the cookie business is incorporated, the legal claim is generally against the corporation, not against Lily personally. In most cases, only the corporation's assets, such as its cash or baking equipment, would be at risk, not Lily's personal savings or home.

As the majority shareholder, Lily continues to run the business and can distribute profits to the other shareholders based on their stock percentages. The difference lies in how the business is structured. Unlike an LLC, a corporation has more formal requirements. Corporate records must be maintained, a defined management structure must be followed, and major decisions must be documented, even when ownership remains concentrated.

This example shows that corporations provide strong liability protection and make it easier to raise money from shareholders, but they also come with more structure and ongoing responsibilities compared to an LLC.

How to Explain to Customers

A corporation gives your business strong legal protection and is ideal if you plan to grow, raise money through investors, or operate in a more formal way. It requires more paperwork and structure than an LLC.
