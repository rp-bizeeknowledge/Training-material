S Corporation (Corporation Under Subchapter S)

Classification for small businesses that can reduce self-employment taxes
What It Is

An S Corp is not a business type formed with the state. It is a tax status chosen with the Internal Revenue Service (IRS). A C Corp or LLC can elect S Corp status by filing IRS Form 2553, which tells the IRS how the business wants to be taxed. Once the IRS approves the election, the business itself does not pay federal income taxes. Instead, profits pass through to the owners and are reported on their personal tax returns.

This structure is why many small business owners choose S Corp taxation. It allows profits to pass through to the owners while separating salary from distributions, which can help reduce overall taxes when handled correctly and in accordance with IRS rules.

TAX BENEFITS FOR LLCs choosing s corp status

Payroll taxes apply only to earned income (the salary an owner pays themselves) not to profit distributions. By structuring compensation this way, active business owners avoid paying the full 15.3% employment tax on every dollar the business earns. TAX BENEFITS FOR C CORPS choosing s corp status

A C Corporation is taxed twice: once at the corporate level, and again when profits are distributed to shareholders as dividends. Electing S Corp status eliminates that second layer by passing income directly to the owner's personal return. Examples
S Corporation Election is Beneficial ✅
Lily's Cookie Stand

Now let's go back to Lily and her homemade cookie stand, but this time the business is formed as a corporation and later elects S Corporation tax status.

The business is doing well and generates steady profits each year. Lily works full time in the business, handling recipes, baking, and operations. As the majority shareholder, she pays herself a reasonable salary, which is subject to payroll taxes. Any remaining profits are distributed to her and the other shareholders as dividends, which are not subject to self-employment tax.

Because the business is profitable and Lily is actively working in it, S Corp taxation helps reduce overall taxes by separating wages from profit distributions. The corporation still provides liability protection, and Lily maintains control while sharing ownership with family members.

In this case, electing S Corp status is beneficial because the business has consistent profits, active owner involvement, and enough income to justify payroll and ongoing compliance.

S Corporation Election is NOT Beneficial ❌
Lily's Cookie Stand

Now imagine Lily forms the cookie business as a corporation but the business is still small and unpredictable in its early stages.

Sales vary month to month, and most of the money goes back into ingredients, equipment, and marketing. The business does not generate enough consistent profit to support paying Lily a reasonable salary. On top of that, payroll setup, ongoing filings, and compliance costs would add extra complexity and expense.

In this scenario, electing S Corp tax status would likely create more administrative burden than tax savings. Lily would still benefit from liability protection as a corporation, but remaining taxed as a C Corp or choosing a simpler structure until the business becomes more profitable would be more practical.

Important IRS Requirements
1
U.S.-Based Entity
The business must be incorporated or organized within the United States.
2
100 Shareholder Limit
An S Corp cannot have more than 100 shareholders at any time.
3
One Class of Stock
Only one class of stock is permitted, though voting rights may differ among shareholders.
4
Eligible Shareholders Only
Must be U.S. residents or qualifying entities. Partnerships and corporations are not eligible.
How to Explain to Customers

An S Corp is a tax status that lets your business avoid paying taxes twice by passing profits straight to your personal tax return. It can save you money on self-employment taxes, but there are rules about who can own the business and how many owners it can have.
