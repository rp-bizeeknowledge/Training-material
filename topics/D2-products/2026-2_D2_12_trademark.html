Trademark Registration

process to protect your brand and valuable company assets such as company name, logo, and tagline
What It Is

Trademark registration gives business owners the exclusive right to use a specific "mark" across the U.S. This could be a business name, slogan, or logo for particular goods or services.

Each of these elements is considered a separate trademark. So, if a customer wants to protect all three, they must complete a separate application for each: one for the name, one for the slogan, and one for the logo. Trademarks help customers recognize the source of a product and legally protect their brand from being copied or misused by others.

Importance

Trademarks play a key role in protecting your brand: the distinct identity that sets your business apart.

Nike

A brand includes elements like your business name (e.g., "Nike"), logo (e.g., the swoosh), and slogan (e.g., "Just Do It"). These elements shape how customers recognize your products and what they associate with your reputation and values.

A trademark legally protects these brand elements. By registering a trademark, you gain the exclusive right to use your name, logo, or slogan in connection with your goods or services. This helps prevent others from using similar marks that could confuse customers or dilute your brand.

Federal registration strengthens this protection by giving you nationwide rights and the ability to take legal action against infringement. It also sends a clear message to consumers: your product or service is authentic, consistent, and backed by a reputable business.

Over time, your trademark becomes a valuable business asset. It can be licensed, sold, or used to increase your brand's market presence and credibility.

Benefits
Protect the brand: A trademark ensures no one else can use your name or logo on similar products. This keeps your brand unique and guards against copycats.
Legal rights: Registering a trademark gives you nationwide rights to that name or logo. You can legally prevent other businesses from using a confusingly similar mark.
Customer trust: Consumers tend to trust products with a known trademark. Seeing a registered mark on a product tells people it's genuine and backed by your company's quality.
Adds value: A strong trademark is a valuable business asset. Well-known marks can increase the worth of your business, and you can license or sell them in the future.

Important Notes

Trademarks registered with the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) remain valid for 10 years, but staying active requires timely maintenance. Owners must file specific documents between the 5th and 6th year after registration, again before the 10-year mark, and every 10 years after that.

To avoid losing your trademark rights, we recommend working with a trademark attorney to ensure all deadlines are met and your registration stays in good standing.

A trademark does not replace the need to file a DBA locally. A DBA is a local or state-level requirement that allows a business to legally operate under a name other than its legal entity name. A federal trademark, filed through the USPTO, offers nationwide brand protection, but it does not authorize business operations under that name in any specific state or locality.

Trademark rights can override conflicting local DBAs. If you own a federally registered trademark, you hold superior rights to that name across the U.S. within your goods/services category. If someone registers a similar or identical DBA at the state or county level, your federal trademark gives you legal grounds to challenge their use, especially if it causes confusion or infringes on your mark. USPTO also makes it clear that first use in commerce (especially with federal registration) often wins in disputes, even if another party registers the name locally.
